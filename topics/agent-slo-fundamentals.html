A framework for defining measurable, business-aligned objectives and a guide to the tools that can help you implement them

Framework for Defining Measurable, Business-Defined Objectives

Defining effective SLOs starts with understanding the business context and translating user expectations into measurable targets. This framework guides you through each step.

1

Identify Critical User Journeys

Start by mapping the key interactions your users have with your service. A Critical User Journey (CUJ) is a sequence of steps a user takes to achieve a specific goal ‚Äî for example, "user logs in and loads their dashboard".

  • Interview product and business stakeholders to surface the most important workflows
  • Prioritise journeys that directly affect business outcomes (revenue, retention, satisfaction)
  • Limit your initial scope to three to five journeys to keep the effort manageable
2

Define Service Level Indicators (SLIs)

An SLI is a quantitative measure of some aspect of the service level being provided. Good SLIs are directly correlated with user experience.

  • Availability: proportion of successful requests over total requests
  • Latency: proportion of requests served within a defined time threshold
  • Throughput: rate of successful operations per unit of time
  • Error rate: proportion of failed requests over total requests
  • Freshness: age of the most recently updated data (for data pipelines)
3

Set Measurable SLO Targets

An SLO is a target value or range for an SLI over a rolling window. Business stakeholders must agree that the target reflects a level of service worth pursuing.

  • Anchor targets to historical performance and user pain thresholds
  • Start slightly below your current measured reliability (avoid setting aspirational, unachievable targets)
  • Use a rolling window (28 days or 30 days) to smooth out short-term noise
  • Document the business rationale for each target so teams understand the "why"
4

Calculate and Manage the Error Budget

The error budget is the amount of unreliability you are permitted before breaching your SLO. It turns reliability into a shared resource that development and operations teams can reason about together.

  • Error budget = 1 ‚àí SLO target (e.g. a 99.9% SLO gives a 0.1% error budget)
  • When the budget is healthy, teams can ship faster; when it is depleted, reliability work takes priority
  • Track burn rate alerts to detect budget depletion before it becomes critical
5

Review and Iterate

SLOs are living agreements. Schedule regular reviews with business and engineering stakeholders to ensure your objectives remain relevant as the service evolves.

  • Review SLOs quarterly or after significant architectural changes
  • Tighten targets when users demand higher reliability and the team has capacity
  • Loosen targets temporarily after major incidents to allow safe recovery space
  • Use post-incident reviews to feed learnings back into SLI definitions

Key Principles for Business-Aligned SLOs

Start with the User

SLOs should reflect the experience of a real user, not internal system metrics. Ask "does this metric capture whether the user successfully achieved their goal?" before adopting an SLI.

Keep It Simple

Fewer, well-chosen SLOs are more actionable than a large number of rarely reviewed ones. Aim for one to three SLOs per Critical User Journey and expand only when the team has built the muscle to manage them.

Make Them Visible

SLOs only drive behaviour when they are visible to the whole team ‚Äî engineers, product managers, and business stakeholders alike. Dashboard radiators, Slack alerts, and weekly digest emails all help keep SLOs top of mind.

Agree on Consequences

Define in advance what happens when the error budget is exhausted. Common policies include freezing feature deployments, convening a reliability review, or escalating to senior leadership. Written policies reduce ambiguity during high-stress incidents.

Google Cloud Monitoring

Native SLO support within Google Cloud lets you define SLOs directly from Cloud Monitoring metrics. Burn rate alerting is built in, making it straightforward to set up multi-window, multi-burn-rate alerts following the Google SRE book recommendations.

  • Integrates natively with Cloud Run, GKE, and App Engine
  • SLO dashboards available out of the box
  • Limited to GCP workloads without additional configuration

Datadog SLOs

Datadog supports three SLO types ‚Äî metric-based, monitor-based, and time-slice ‚Äî giving teams flexibility based on their data model. SLO widgets integrate with dashboards and notebooks for stakeholder reporting.

  • SLO status pages for sharing reliability with non-technical stakeholders
  • Composite SLOs allow aggregating multiple services into a single target
  • API-first design enables GitOps-style SLO management

Prometheus + Sloth

Sloth is an open-source tool that generates SLO-compliant Prometheus recording rules and alerts from a simple YAML specification. It follows the multi-window, multi-burn-rate alerting approach recommended by Google.

  • Fully open source with no licensing costs
  • GitOps friendly ‚Äî SLO definitions live alongside your service code
  • Requires an existing Prometheus and Alertmanager setup

Nobl9

Nobl9 is a dedicated SLO platform that acts as a unified layer across multiple observability backends. It supports the OpenSLO specification for portable SLO definitions and provides a purpose-built UI for reliability reviews.

  • Connects to over 30 data sources including Prometheus, Datadog, and Splunk
  • Annotations allow teams to correlate SLO dips with deployments and incidents
  • Commercial product with a free tier for small teams

OpenSLO

OpenSLO is a CNCF-aligned open specification for defining SLOs in a vendor-neutral YAML format. It is not a tool in itself, but an interoperability standard that tools like Nobl9 and Sloth can consume.

  • Prevents vendor lock-in by keeping SLO definitions portable
  • Growing ecosystem of converters and integrations
  • Ideal as a source-of-truth format checked into version control

How to Choose the Right Tool

1

Assess Your Stack

If you are already invested in a platform like Datadog or Google Cloud, start with its native SLO features to reduce operational overhead. You can always migrate to a more portable format later.

2

Consider Portability

Teams operating across multiple cloud providers or with a diverse observability stack benefit from a dedicated SLO layer (Nobl9) or an open standard (OpenSLO) to avoid fragmented SLO definitions.

3

Evaluate Cost and Scale

Open-source solutions like Prometheus + Sloth are cost-effective at scale but require operational investment. Commercial platforms offer managed experiences that reduce toil but add licensing costs.
